**区块链**（ 或
）\[1\]是用[分布式数据库识别](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/分布式数据库 "wikilink")、传播和记载信息的智能化[对等网络](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/对等网络 "wikilink")，也称为价值互联网。\[2\]\[3\]
[中本聪在](../Page/中本聪.md "wikilink")2008年，于《[比特币白皮书](../Page/比特币.md "wikilink")》\[4\]中提出“区块链”概念，并在2009年创立了，开发出第一个区块，即“创世区块”。\[5\]

区块链共享价值体系首先被众多的[加密貨幣效仿](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/加密貨幣 "wikilink")，并在[工作量证明上和算法上进行了改进](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/工作量证明 "wikilink")，如采用[权益证明和](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/权益证明 "wikilink")。随后，区块链生态系统在全球不断进化，出现了[首次代币发售ICO](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/首次代币发售 "wikilink")、智能合约区块链[以太坊](../Page/以太坊.md "wikilink")、“轻所有权、重使用权”的资产代币化[共享经济](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/共享经济 "wikilink")\[6\]
以及。目前，人们正在利用这一共享价值体系，在各行各业开发去中心化电脑程序(Decentralized applications,
Dapp)\[7\]，在全球各地构建和去中心化自主社区(Decentralized autonomous society, DAS)\[8\]。

A **blockchain**, originally **block chain**, is a continuously growing
list of
[records](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Record_\(computer_science\) "wikilink"),
called *blocks*, which are linked and secured using
[cryptography](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/cryptography "wikilink").\[36\]\[37\]
Each block contains a [cryptographic
hash](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptographic_hash_function "wikilink")
of the previous block,\[38\] a
[timestamp](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trusted_timestamping "wikilink"),
and transaction data (generally represented as a [merkle
tree](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/merkle_tree "wikilink") root hash).
By design, a blockchain is resistant to modification of the data. It is
"an open, [distributed
ledger](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/distributed_ledger "wikilink")
that can record transactions between two parties efficiently and in a
verifiable and permanent way".\[39\] For use as a distributed
[ledger](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/ledger "wikilink"), a blockchain
is typically managed by a
[peer-to-peer](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/peer-to-peer "wikilink")
network collectively adhering to a
[protocol](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protocol_\(communication\) "wikilink")
for inter-node communication and validating new blocks. Once recorded,
the data in any given block cannot be altered retroactively without
alteration of all subsequent blocks, which requires consensus of the
network majority.

**區塊鏈**（或）是藉用密碼學\[40\]\[41\]串接並保護其內容的串連交易[記錄](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/記錄 "wikilink")（又稱區塊）。每一個區塊包含了前一個區塊的[加密雜湊](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/密碼雜湊函數 "wikilink")、對應的時間戳記以及交易資料（通常用演算法計算的雜湊值表示），這樣的設計使得區塊內容具有難以被竄改的特性。用區塊鏈所串接的分散式帳本能讓兩方有效率地紀錄交易，且此交易可永久被查驗。區塊鏈使用的[分散式帳本通常由](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/分散式帳本 "wikilink")[點對點網路中內部節點的通訊協定所共同管理](../Page/對等網路.md "wikilink")，並且讓新的區塊生效。一旦帳本有新的交易紀錄產生，交易資料在該區塊中便不得被回溯替換，除非所有接續的區塊都一併產生替換值，而這需要取得網路的多數決同意才能達成。

Blockchains are [secure by
design](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/secure_by_design "wikilink") and
exemplify a distributed computing system with high [Byzantine fault
tolerance](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_fault_tolerance "wikilink").
[Decentralized](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decentralized "wikilink")
consensus has therefore been achieved with a blockchain.\[42\] This
makes blockchains potentially suitable for the recording of events,
medical records,\[43\]\[44\] and other [records
management](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/records_management "wikilink")
activities, such as [identity
management](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/identity_management "wikilink"),\[45\]\[46\]\[47\]
[transaction
processing](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/transaction_processing "wikilink"),
documenting
[provenance](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/provenance "wikilink"), [food
traceability](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traceability#Food_processing "wikilink")\[48\],
and voting.\[49\]

區塊鏈使用拜占庭容錯機制來確保分散式計算系統的安全與一致性，去中心化的決議也因此能在區塊鏈裡實現。\[50\]
這使得區塊鏈具有事件紀錄、醫學紀錄，或其他記錄管理，像是：身份管理、交易處理、文件出處、食品來源、投票等應用的潛力。

## 歷史History

[Confirmed_Transactions_Per_Day.png](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Confirmed_Transactions_Per_Day.png "fig:Confirmed_Transactions_Per_Day.png")
The first work on a cryptographically secured chain of blocks was
described in 1991 by Stuart Haber and W. Scott Stornetta.\[51\]\[52\]
They wanted to implement a system where documents' timestamps could not
be tampered with or backdated. In 1992, Bayer, Haber and Stornetta
incorporated [Merkle
trees](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merkle_tree "wikilink") to the
design, which improved its efficiency by allowing several documents to
be collected into one block.\[53\]\[54\]

The first blockchain was conceptualized by a person (or group of people)
known as [Satoshi
Nakamoto](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satoshi_Nakamoto "wikilink") in
2008. It was implemented the following year by Nakamoto as a core
component of the cryptocurrency bitcoin, where it serves as the public
[ledger](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/ledger "wikilink") for all
transactions on the network. Through the use of a blockchain, bitcoin
became the first digital currency to solve the
[double-spending](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/double-spending "wikilink")
problem without requiring a trusted authority and has been the
inspiration for many additional applications.

In August 2014, the bitcoin blockchain file size, containing records of
all transactions that have occurred on the network, reached 20 GB
([gigabytes](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/gigabyte "wikilink")).\[55\]
In January 2015, the size had grown to almost 30 GB, and from January
2016 to January 2017, the bitcoin blockchain grew from 50 GB to 100 GB
in size.\[56\]

The words *block* and *chain* were used separately in Satoshi Nakamoto's
original paper, but were eventually popularized as a single word,
*blockchain,* by 2016. The term *blockchain 2.0* refers to new
applications of the distributed blockchain database, first emerging in
2014.\[57\] *[The
Economist](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Economist "wikilink")*
described one implementation of this second-generation programmable
blockchain as coming with "a programming language that allows users to
write more sophisticated smart contracts, thus creating invoices that
pay themselves when a shipment arrives or share certificates which
automatically send their owners dividends if profits reach a certain
level." Blockchain 2.0 technologies go beyond transactions and enable
"exchange of value without powerful intermediaries acting as arbiters of
money and information ." They are expected to enable excluded people to
enter the global economy, protect the privacy of participants, allow
people to "monetize their own information," and provide the capability
to ensure creators are compensated for their [intellectual
property](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/intellectual_property "wikilink").
Second-generation blockchain technology makes it possible to store an
individual's "persistent digital ID and persona" and provides an avenue
to help solve the problem of [social
inequality](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/social_inequality "wikilink")
by "potentially changing the way wealth is distributed".\[58\] ,
blockchain 2.0 implementations continue to require an off-chain
[oracle](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oracle_\(computer_science\) "wikilink")
to access any "external data or events based on time or market
conditions \[that need\] to interact with the blockchain."\[59\]

In 2016, the central securities depository of the Russian Federation
([NSD](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Settlement_Depository_\(Russia\) "wikilink"))
announced a pilot project, based on the [Nxt](../Page/Nxt.md "wikilink")
blockchain 2.0 platform, that would explore the use of blockchain-based
automated voting systems.\[60\] IBM opened a blockchain innovation
research center in Singapore in July 2016.\[61\] A working group for the
[World Economic
Forum](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Economic_Forum "wikilink")
met in November 2016 to discuss the development of [governance
models](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multistakeholder_governance_model "wikilink")
related to blockchain.\[62\] According to
[Accenture](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accenture "wikilink"), an
application of the [diffusion of
innovations](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/diffusion_of_innovations "wikilink")
theory suggests that blockchains attained a 13.5% adoption rate within
financial services in 2016, therefore reaching the [early
adopters](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/early_adopter "wikilink")
phase.\[63\] Industry trade groups joined to create the Global
Blockchain Forum in 2016, an initiative of the [Chamber of Digital
Commerce](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chamber_of_Digital_Commerce "wikilink").\[64\]

In May 2018, [Gartner](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gartner "wikilink")
found that only 1% of CIOs indicated any kind of blockchain adoption
within their organisations, and only 8% of CIOs were in the short-term
‘planning or \[looking at\] active experimentation with

## 架構Structure

A blockchain is a decentralized, distributed and public digital ledger
that is used to record transactions across many computers so that the
record cannot be altered retroactively without the alteration of all
subsequent blocks and the consensus of the network.\[66\] This allows
the participants to verify and audit transactions inexpensively.\[67\] A
blockchain database is managed autonomously using a
[peer-to-peer](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/peer-to-peer "wikilink")
network and a distributed timestamping server. They are
[authenticated](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authentication "wikilink")
by [mass
collaboration](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/mass_collaboration "wikilink")
powered by
[collective](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/collective "wikilink")
[self-interests](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/self-interest "wikilink").\[68\]
The result is a
[robust](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/robustness_\(computer_science\) "wikilink")
[workflow](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/workflow "wikilink") where
participants' uncertainty regarding data security is marginal. The use
of a blockchain removes the characteristic of infinite
[reproducibility](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproduction_\(economics\) "wikilink")
from a digital asset. It confirms that each unit of value was
transferred only once, solving the long-standing problem of [double
spending](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/double_spending "wikilink").
Blockchains have been described as a
[value](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/value_\(economics\) "wikilink")-exchange
[protocol](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptographic_protocol "wikilink").\[69\]
This blockchain-based exchange of value can be completed quicker, safer
and cheaper than with traditional systems.\[70\] A blockchain can assign
[title](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/title_\(property\) "wikilink")
rights because, when properly set up to detail the exchange agreement,
it provides a record that compels [offer and
acceptance](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/offer_and_acceptance "wikilink").\[71\]\[72\]

區塊鏈是一個去中心化、分散式且公開的數位帳本，用來記錄多台電腦間的交易，以避免記錄在沒有取得所有接續區塊的修正資訊與網路共識的情況下被回溯修改。這樣的特性提供參與者得已用不昂貴的方式去驗證及審核交易過程。區塊鏈資料庫藉由點對點網路與分散式的時間戳記伺服器而自主管理。他們可被?的大量合作而驗證，因而在資料能被保護的情況下，便能在最低限度的參與者不確定性中提供一個穩定的工作流程。

### Blocks

Blocks hold batches of valid
[transactions](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transaction_processing "wikilink")
that are hashed and encoded into a [Merkle
tree](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merkle_tree "wikilink").\[73\] Each
block includes the [cryptographic
hash](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/cryptographic_hash "wikilink") of
the prior block in the blockchain, linking the two. The linked blocks
form a chain.\[74\] This
[iterative](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iteration "wikilink") process
confirms the integrity of the previous block, all the way back to the
original genesis block.\[75\]

Sometimes separate blocks can be produced concurrently, creating a
temporary fork. In addition to a secure hash-based history, any
blockchain has a specified algorithm for scoring different versions of
the history so that one with a higher value can be selected over others.
Blocks not selected for inclusion in the chain are called orphan
blocks.\[76\] Peers supporting the database have different versions of
the history from time to time. They keep only the highest-scoring
version of the database known to them. Whenever a peer receives a
higher-scoring version (usually the old version with a single new block
added) they extend or overwrite their own database and retransmit the
improvement to their peers. There is never an absolute guarantee that
any particular entry will remain in the best version of the history
forever. Because blockchains are typically built to add the score of new
blocks onto old blocks and because there are incentives to work only on
extending with new blocks rather than overwriting old blocks, the
probability of an entry becoming superseded goes down
exponentially\[77\] as more blocks are built on top of it, eventually
becoming very low. For example, in a blockchain using the [proof-of-work
system](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/proof-of-work_system "wikilink"),
the chain with the most cumulative proof-of-work is always considered
the valid one by the network. There are a number of methods that can be
used to demonstrate a sufficient level of
[computation](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/computation "wikilink").
Within a blockchain the computation is carried out redundantly rather
than in the traditional segregated and
[parallel](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_computing "wikilink")
manner.\[78\]

#### Block time

The *block time* is the average time it takes for the network to
generate one extra block in the blockchain.\[79\] Some blockchains
create a new block as frequently as every five seconds.\[80\] By the
time of block completion, the included data becomes verifiable. In
cryptocurrency, this is practically when the transaction takes place, so
a shorter block time means faster transactions. The block time for
[Ethereum](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethereum "wikilink") is set to
between 14 and 15 seconds, while for bitcoin it is 10 minutes.\[81\]

### Decentralization

By storing data across its peer-to-peer network, the blockchain
eliminates a number of risks that come with data being held centrally.
The decentralized blockchain may use
[ad-hoc](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/ad-hoc "wikilink") [message
passing](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/message_passing "wikilink") and
[distributed
networking](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/distributed_networking "wikilink").

Peer-to-peer blockchain networks lack centralized points of
vulnerability that [computer
crackers](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Security_hacker "wikilink") can
exploit; likewise, it has no central point of
[failure](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/failure "wikilink"). Blockchain
security methods include the use of [public-key
cryptography](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/public-key_cryptography "wikilink").
A *public key* (a long, random-looking string of numbers) is an address
on the blockchain. Value tokens sent across the network are recorded as
belonging to that address. A *private key* is like a password that gives
its owner access to their digital assets or the means to otherwise
interact with the various capabilities that blockchains now support.
Data stored on the blockchain is generally considered incorruptible.

Every
[node](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Node_\(networking\) "wikilink") in
a decentralized system has a copy of the blockchain. [Data
quality](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_quality "wikilink") is
maintained by massive database
[replication](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Replication_\(computing\) "wikilink")\[82\]
and [computational
trust](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/computational_trust "wikilink"). No
centralized "official" copy exists and no user is "trusted" more than
any other. Transactions are broadcast to the network using software.
Messages are delivered on a
[best-effort](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Best-effort_delivery "wikilink")
basis. Mining nodes validate transactions,\[83\] add them to the block
they are building, and then
[broadcast](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Broadcasting_\(networking\) "wikilink")
the completed block to other nodes. Blockchains use various
time-stamping schemes, such as
[proof-of-work](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof-of-work_system "wikilink"),
to serialize changes. Alternate consensus methods include
[proof-of-stake](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/proof-of-stake "wikilink").\[84\]
Growth of a decentralized blockchain is accompanied by the risk of node
centralization because the computer resources required to process larger
amounts of data become more expensive.\[85\]

### Openness

Open blockchains are more
[user-friendly](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/usability "wikilink") than
some traditional ownership records, which, while open to the public,
still require physical access to view. Because all early blockchains
were permissionless, controversy has arisen over the blockchain
definition. An issue in this ongoing debate is whether a private system
with verifiers tasked and authorized (permissioned) by a central
authority should be considered a blockchain. Proponents of permissioned
or private chains argue that the term "blockchain" may be applied to any
data structure that batches data into time-stamped blocks. These
blockchains serve as a distributed version of [multiversion concurrency
control](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/multiversion_concurrency_control "wikilink")
(MVCC) in databases. Just as MVCC prevents two transactions from
concurrently modifying a single object in a database, blockchains
prevent two transactions from spending the same single output in a
blockchain.\[86\] Opponents say that permissioned systems resemble
traditional corporate databases, not supporting decentralized data
verification, and that such systems are not hardened against operator
tampering and revision. Nikolai Hampton of
*[Computerworld](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computerworld "wikilink")*
said that "many in-house blockchain solutions will be nothing more than
cumbersome databases," and "without a clear security model, proprietary
blockchains should be eyed with suspicion."\[87\] Business analysts [Don
Tapscott](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don_Tapscott "wikilink") and
[Alex Tapscott](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alex_Tapscott "wikilink")
define blockchain as a distributed ledger or database open to anyone.

#### Permissionless

The great advantage to an open, permissionless, or public, blockchain
network is that guarding against bad actors is not required and no
[access
control](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/access_control "wikilink") is
needed.\[88\] This means that applications can be added to the network
without the approval or trust of others, using the blockchain as a
[transport
layer](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/transport_layer "wikilink").\[89\]

Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies currently secure their blockchain by
requiring new entries to include a proof of work. To prolong the
blockchain, bitcoin uses
[Hashcash](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hashcash "wikilink") puzzles.
While Hashcash was designed in 1997 by [Adam
Back](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Back "wikilink"), the original
idea was first proposed by [Cynthia
Dwork](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cynthia_Dwork "wikilink") and [Moni
Naor](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moni_Naor "wikilink") and Eli
Ponyatovski in their 1992 paper "Pricing via Processing or Combatting
Junk Mail".

Financial companies have not prioritised decentralized
blockchains.\[90\] In 2016, [venture
capital](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/venture_capital "wikilink")
investment for blockchain-related projects was weakening in the USA but
increasing in China.\[91\] Bitcoin and many other cryptocurrencies use
open (public) blockchains. , bitcoin has the highest market
capitalization.

#### Permissioned (private) blockchain

Permissioned blockchains use an access control layer to govern who has
access to the network.\[92\] In contrast to public blockchain networks,
validators on private blockchain networks are vetted by the network
owner. They do not rely on anonymous nodes to validate transactions nor
do they benefit from the [network
effect](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/network_effect "wikilink").\[93\]
Permissioned blockchains can also go by the name of 'consortium' or
'hybrid' blockchains.\[94\]

The *[New York
Times](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_Times "wikilink")* noted
in both 2016 and 2017 that many corporations are using blockchain
networks "with private blockchains, independent of the public
system."\[95\]\[96\]

#### Disadvantages of private blockchain

Nikolai Hampton pointed out in
*[Computerworld](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computerworld "wikilink")*
that "There is also no need for a '51 percent' attack on a private
blockchain, as the private blockchain (most likely) already controls 100
percent of all block creation resources. If you could attack or damage
the blockchain creation tools on a private corporate server, you could
effectively control 100 percent of their network and alter transactions
however you wished."\[97\] This has a set of particularly profound
adverse implications during a [financial
crisis](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/financial_crisis "wikilink") or
[debt crisis](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/debt_crisis "wikilink") like
the [financial crisis of
2007–08](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/financial_crisis_of_2007–08 "wikilink"),
where politically powerful actors may make decisions that favor some
groups at the expense of others\[98\]\[99\], and "the bitcoin blockchain
is protected by the massive group mining effort. It's unlikely that any
private blockchain will try to protect records using gigawatts of
computing power—it's time consuming and expensive."\[100\] He also said,
"Within a private blockchain there is also no 'race'; there's no
incentive to use more power or discover blocks faster than competitors.
This means that many in-house blockchain solutions will be nothing more
than cumbersome databases."\[101\]

### General potentials

Blockchain technology has a large potential to transform business
[operating
models](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/operating_model "wikilink") in the
long term. Blockchain distributed ledger technology is more a
[foundational
technology](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundational_innovation "wikilink")—with
the potential to create new foundations for global economic and social
systems—than a [disruptive
technology](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/disruptive_technology "wikilink"),
which typically "attack a traditional business model with a lower-cost
solution and overtake incumbent firms quickly".\[104\] Even so, there
are a few operational products maturing from [proof of
concept](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/proof_of_concept "wikilink") by
late 2016.\[105\] The use of blockchains promises to bring significant
efficiencies to global [supply
chains](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/supply_chain "wikilink"),
financial transactions, asset ledgers and decentralized social
networking.\[106\]

, some observers remain skeptical. Steve Wilson, of Constellation
Research, believes the technology has been
[hyped](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/media_circus "wikilink") with
unrealistic claims.\[107\] To mitigate
[risk](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/risk_management "wikilink"),
businesses are reluctant to place blockchain at the core of the business
structure.\[108\]

This means specific blockchain applications may be a disruptive
innovation, because substantially lower-cost solutions can be
instantiated, which can disrupt existing business models.\[109\]
Blockchain protocols facilitate businesses to use new methods of
processing digital transactions.\[110\] Examples include a payment
system and [digital
currency](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/digital_currency "wikilink"),
facilitating
[crowdsales](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/crowdfunding "wikilink"), or
implementing [prediction
markets](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/prediction_market "wikilink") and
generic
[governance](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/governance "wikilink")
tools.\[111\]

Blockchains alleviate the need for a [trust service
provider](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/trust_service_provider "wikilink")
and are predicted to result in less
[capital](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/capital_\(economics\) "wikilink")
being tied up in disputes. Blockchains have the potential to reduce
[systemic risk](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/systemic_risk "wikilink")
and financial [fraud](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/fraud "wikilink").
They automate processes that were previously time-consuming and done
manually, such as the
[incorporation](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/incorporation_\(business\) "wikilink")
of businesses.\[112\] In theory, it would be possible to collect taxes,
conduct conveyancing and provide risk management with blockchains.

As a distributed ledger, blockchain reduces the costs involved in
verifying transactions, and by removing the need for trusted
"third-parties" such as banks to complete transactions, the technology
also lowers the cost of networking, therefore allowing several
applications.\[113\]

Starting with a strong focus on financial applications, blockchain
technology is extending to activities including decentralized
applications and collaborative organizations that eliminate a
middleman.\[114\]

### Smart contracts

Blockchain-based [smart
contracts](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/smart_contract "wikilink") are
contracts that can be partially or fully executed or enforced without
human interaction.\[125\] One of the main objectives of a smart contract
is [automated](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/automation "wikilink")
[escrow](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/escrow "wikilink"). The
[IMF](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund "wikilink")
believes smart contracts based on blockchain technology could reduce
[moral hazards](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/moral_hazard "wikilink")
and optimize the use of contracts in general.\[126\] Due to the lack of
widespread use their legal status is unclear.\[127\]

Some blockchain implementations could enable the coding of contracts
that will execute when specified conditions are met. A blockchain smart
contract would be enabled by
[extensible](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/extensible_programming "wikilink")
programming instructions that define and execute an agreement.\[128\]
For example, [Ethereum Solidity](../Page/Solidity.md "wikilink") is an
open-source blockchain project that was built specifically to realize
this possibility by implementing a
[Turing-complete](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turing_complete "wikilink")
programming language capability to implement such contracts.\[129\]

A number of financial institutions have adopted blockchain
technology-based money transactions using smart contracts. In May 2018,
[Sberbank CIB](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sberbank_CIB "wikilink"),
the corporate and investment banking unit of Russia's largest bank
announced that it conducted, in cooperation with a leading Russian
telecom operator and the National Settlement Depository (NSD), a $12
billion transaction for the placement of
[bonds](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/bond_\(finance\) "wikilink") with
settlement in [roubels](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/roubel "wikilink")
based on smart contracts.\[130\]

## Types of blockchains

Currently, there are three types of blockchain networks - public
blockchains, private blockchains and
[consortium](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/consortium "wikilink")
blockchains.\[193\]

### Public blockchains

A public blockchain has absolutely no access restrictions. Anyone with
an [internet](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/internet "wikilink")
connection can send
[transactions](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Financial_transaction "wikilink")
to it as well as become a
[validator](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/validator "wikilink") (i.e.,
participate in the execution of a [consensus
protocol](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consensus_\(computer_science\) "wikilink")).
\[194\] Usually, such networks offer [economic
incentives](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incentive "wikilink") for
those who secure them and utilize some type of a [Proof of
Stake](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof-of-stake "wikilink") or
[Proof of
Work](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof-of-work_system "wikilink")
algorithm.

Some of the largest, most known public blockchains are Bitcoin and
Ethereum.

### Private blockchains

A private blockchain is permissioned.\[195\] One cannot join it unless
invited by the network administrators. Participant and validator access
is restricted.\[196\]

This type of blockchains can be considered a middle-ground for companies
that are interested in the blockchain technology in general but are not
comfortable with a level of control offered by public networks.
Typically, they seek to
[incorporate](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incorporation_\(business\) "wikilink")
blockchain into their accounting and record-keeping procedures without
sacrificing autonomy and running the risk of exposing sensitive data to
the public internet.

### Consortium blockchains

A consortium blockchain is often said to be semi-decentralized. It, too,
is permissioned but instead of a single
[organization](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/organization "wikilink")
controlling it, a number of companies might each operate a
[node](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Node_\(networking\) "wikilink") on
such a network. The administrators of a consortium chain restrict users’
reading rights as they see fit and only allow a limited set of trusted
nodes to execute a consensus protocol.\[197\]

## Academic research

[BlockchainPanelDiscussionAtIEEETechIgnite2017.jpg](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:BlockchainPanelDiscussionAtIEEETechIgnite2017.jpg "fig:BlockchainPanelDiscussionAtIEEETechIgnite2017.jpg")
TechIgnite conference\]\]

In October 2014, the MIT Bitcoin Club, with funding from MIT alumni,
provided undergraduate students at the [Massachusetts Institute of
Technology](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology "wikilink")
access to $100 of bitcoin. The adoption rates, as studied by Catalini
and Tucker (2016), revealed that when people who typically adopt
technologies early are given delayed access, they tend to reject the
technology.\[198\]

### Journals

In September 2015, the first peer-reviewed academic journal dedicated to
cryptocurrency and blockchain technology research, *Ledger*, was
announced. The inaugural issue was published in December
2016.\[199\]\[200\] The journal covers aspects of
[mathematics](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/mathematics "wikilink"),
[computer
science](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/computer_science "wikilink"),
[engineering](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/engineering "wikilink"),
[law](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/law "wikilink"),
[economics](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/economics "wikilink") and
[philosophy](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/philosophy "wikilink") that
relate to cryptocurrencies such as bitcoin.\[201\]\[202\]

The journal encourages authors to [digitally
sign](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_signature "wikilink") a
[file hash](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hash_function "wikilink") of
submitted papers, which will then be
[timestamped](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trusted_timestamping "wikilink")
into the bitcoin blockchain. Authors are also asked to include a
personal bitcoin address in the first page of their papers.\[203\]

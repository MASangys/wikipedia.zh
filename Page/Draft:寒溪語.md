**寒溪語**（）或稱**宜蘭克里奧爾語**，[Japanese](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_language "wikilink")-based
[creole](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/creole_language "wikilink") of
Taiwan. It arose in the 1930s and 1940s, with contact between Japanese
colonists and the native [Atayal
people](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atayal_people "wikilink") of
southern [Yilan County,
Taiwan](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yilan_County,_Taiwan "wikilink").
The vocabulary of a speaker born in 1974 was 70% Japanese and 30%
[Atayal](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atayal_language "wikilink"), but
the grammar of the creole does not closely resemble either of the source
languages.\[1\]

It is incomprehensible to both
[Japanese](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_language "wikilink")
and [Atayal](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atayal_language "wikilink")
native speakers.\[2\] The
[creole](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creole_language "wikilink") was
identified in 2006 by Chien Yuehchen and Sanada Shinji, however its
existence is still largely unknown.\[3\]\[4\] It was named by Sanada and
Chien for its location.\[5\] The [official
language](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/official_language "wikilink") of
[Taiwan](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "wikilink"),
[Mandarin](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese "wikilink"),
threatens the existence of Yilan Creole.\[6\]

## Classification

Yilan Creole is a creole language that is considered to be part of the
Japonic language family.\[7\] The superstratum and substratum languages
of the creole are Japanese and Atayal, respectively.\[8\] It has
possibly been used as the first language among the Atayal and Seediq
people since the 1930s.\[9\]

## History

The island of [Taiwan was annexed by
Japan](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_under_Japanese_rule "wikilink")
under the [Shimonoseki
Treaty](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Shimonoseki "wikilink")
of 1895 until 1945.\[10\]\[11\] Control over the island lasted about
fifty years.\[12\] During the latter period of this time, [Imperial
Japan](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_of_Japan "wikilink")
enforced Taiwanese
[assimilation](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_assimilation "wikilink")
to Japanese language and culture.\[13\] As a result of this contact
between the
[Atayal](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atayal_language "wikilink") and
[Japanese](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_language "wikilink")
languages, Yilan Creole surfaced.\[14\] Taiwanese people began attending
schools taught in Japanese where all non-Japanese languages were banned,
and by 1944 over 77% of Taiwanese were capable in speaking
Japanese.\[15\]\[16\] Language reforms, name changes, and laws regarding
social customs were among the reforms instilled by the Japanese Imperial
government.\[17\] Many are still competent in Japanese today where it is
sometimes used as a [lingua
franca](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/lingua_franca "wikilink").\[18\]\[19\]
Although China attempted to rid influence of Japan in Taiwan after the
[surrender of
Japan](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/surrender_of_Japan "wikilink") in
1945, the impact on language and culture in Taiwan is still largely
evident.\[20\]
[Taiwan_ROC_political_division_map_Yilan_County.svg](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Taiwan_ROC_political_division_map_Yilan_County.svg "fig:Taiwan_ROC_political_division_map_Yilan_County.svg")
Atayal features surfaced in the Japanese language spoken in Taiwan,
eventually becoming a
[pidgin](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/pidgin "wikilink") before it
fully developed into a
[creole](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creole_language "wikilink"), and
the language is now currently the only known Japanese-based creole on
the island, and possibly in the world.\[21\]\[22\]

## Geographic Distribution

The [creole](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creole_language "wikilink")
is spoke in [Yilan
County](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yilan_County,_Taiwan "wikilink")
in Eastern Taiwan, mainly in Tungyueh Village, Chinyang Village, Aohua
Village, and Hanhsi Village, with evidence of differences
[varieties](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variety_\(linguistics\) "wikilink")
of the creole found in each.\[23\]\[24\] Although the exact number of
Yilan Creole speakers is unknown, it is likely less than the total
population of the four villages, which is 3,000.\[25\]\[26\] One
estimate is 2,000 to 3,000 speakers of Yilan Creole currently
exist.\[27\] While the creole is currently used among all
generations,\[28\] younger generations are receiving less exposure to
it, causing the language to become
[endangered](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endangered_language "wikilink").\[29\]
While older speakers may not be fluent in
[Mandarin](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese "wikilink"),
younger generations are consistently using Mandarin more.\[30\]

Japanese language still has some influence in Taiwan society today.
Japanese based Internet sites in Japanese are viewed by Taiwanese, with
“few other countries \[producing\] as much information in Japanese as
Taiwan”.\[31\] Evidence of Japanese signboards, particularly with the
use of the Japanese
[hiragana](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/hiragana "wikilink") character
の (pronounced “no”) may be seen in Taiwan.\[32\] Taiwanese citizens
who received Japanese education during the annexation of the nation
still speak Japanese fluently today.\[33\] There are three generations
of Yilan Creole speakers, with the older and middle generations using
the creole significantly more than the younger generation.\[34\]

In Tungyueh Village, younger generations seem to have lost the Yilan
Creole, while there are still fluent younger generation speakers of the
Creole in Aohua.\[35\] After a push for preserving a more traditional
and pure sense of [Atayal
heritage](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atayal_people "wikilink"), the
Yilan Creole that is imbued with Japanese features was removed from
language examinations. This further instilled a tendency toward Mandarin
and a push away from Yilan Creole in young speakers.\[36\] While older
generations prefer to speak Japanese or Yilan-Creole with people of the
same age, they will often use Atayal or Mandarin mixed with Yilan Creole
when speaking with younger generations; younger generations will
similarly prefer Mandarin with same-age speakers, but may use Yilan
Creole with older generations.\[37\]

Due to the migration of Japanese from the western part of the Japan to
Taiwan in the early half of the twentieth century, with seventy percent
of Taiwan’s immigrants being from western Japan, Yilan Creole acquired
some of the regional dialects of western Japan. However, these [regional
dialects](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect "wikilink") are still
used unconsciously.\[38\]

Japanese consonants that Yilan Creole has inherited include the
[voiced](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voice_\(phonetics\) "wikilink")
[stops](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stop_consonant "wikilink") \[b\],
\[d\] and \[g\]**,** voiced alveolar
[fricative](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fricative_consonant "wikilink")
\[z\]**,**
[alveo-palatal](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alveolo-palatal_consonant "wikilink")
fricative \[ɕ\]**,** alveolar
[affricate](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affricate_consonant "wikilink")
\[ts\], and alveo-palatal affricates \[tɕ\] and \[dʑ\]. It does not have
the
[bilabial](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilabial_consonant "wikilink")
fricative \[Φ\] and
[uvular](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uvular_consonant "wikilink")
[nasal](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasal_consonant "wikilink") \[N\]
found in Japanese, however.\[40\]

Atayal Consonants the Yilan Creole has inherited include the [glottal
stop](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/glottal_stop "wikilink") \[ʔ\],
alveolar
[liquid](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_consonant "wikilink")
\[l\], [velar](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velar_consonant "wikilink")
fricative \[x\], and the liquid \[l\]. It does not have the uvular stop
\[q\] of Atayal. Some other features that Yilan Creole adopted from
Atayal are the consonants \[t\] and \[k\] can occur word-finally, velar
nasal \[ŋ\] can appear in the word-initial also word-final position, and
fricatives \[s\], \[x\] and \[h\] can occur in word-final position. In
some Japanese-based words, \[l\] has taken place of \[r\], such as
*suware* ‘to sit’ becoming *suwale* ‘to sit’ in Yilan Creole. The
[phoneme](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/phoneme "wikilink") \[l\]
similarly does this with \[d\] in many Japanese based words.\[41\]

### Vowels

Yilan Creole [vowels](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/vowel "wikilink")
consist of \[a\], \[i\], \[u\], \[e\] and \[o\] which derive from both
Japanese and Atayal, but also \[ə\] which derives specifically from
Atayal.\[42\] However, \[u\] in Yilan Creole resembles the
[rounded](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roundedness "wikilink") \[u\] of
Atayal instead of the unrounded \[ɯ\] in Japanese.\[43\]

Long consonants and vowels from Japanese words are often shortened in
Yilan Creole, where *gakkô* ‘school’ in Japanese becomes *gako* ‘school’
in Yilan Creole.\[44\]

### Stress

[Stress](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress_\(linguistics\) "wikilink")
in Yilan Creole falls on the final syllable as it does in the Atayal
language.\[45\]

## Grammar

### Morphology

Although Yilan Creole verbs derive from Japanese and Ayatal, [verb
conjugation](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation "wikilink")
patterns uniquely differ in some aspects.
[Tense](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tense–aspect–mood "wikilink") is
noted through the use of
[affixes](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/affix "wikilink") and temporal
adverbs together. Ayatal based verbs will still use Japanese
affixation.\[46\] Some processes of negation in Yilan Creole use
Japanese derived forms to accommodate the [realis
mood](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/realis_mood "wikilink") that is part
of Atayal grammar.\[47\]

### Syntax

The [word order](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/word_order "wikilink") of
Yilan Creole is SOV. It follows Japanese sentence structure, however,
there is evidence of Mandarin-based SVO sentences as well, particularly
in younger speakers.\[48\]

## Vocabulary/Lexis

In Yilan Creole, phonological forms of words are derived from Japanese,
while the [semantic](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantics "wikilink")
properties are derived from Atayal.\[49\] According to a study on Yilan
Creole in Tungyueh Village by Zeitoun, Teng, and Wu, “the proportion of
Atayal-derived words in the Yilan Creole basic vocabulary is 18.3%, and
that of Japanese-derived words is 35.6%. Both Atayal-derived words and
Japanese-derived words can be used for 33.8% of the items”.\[50\]
[Mandarin](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese "wikilink")
and [Southern
Min](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Min "wikilink") words also
exist, but far less. Older generation speakers tend to use Atayal and
Japanese variants more often than younger generational speakers, who
prefer Mandarin variants, when they exist. Mandarin based words lose
their tone in Yilan Creole.\[51\]

Many Atayal words relating to nature, animals, and plants survived in
the creole. Vocabulary of most concepts such as these related to
traditional
[Atayal](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atayal_people "wikilink") and
[Seediq](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seediq_people "wikilink") life
and culture are retained in Yilan Creole.\[52\]

### Suffixes

The verb [suffix](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/suffix "wikilink")
*–suru* in Yilan Creole**',** derived from the Japanese verb ‘to do’,
is similar to its Japanese counterpart, except in that it is a [bound
morpheme](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bound_and_unbound_morphemes "wikilink")
while the Japanese *-suru* can stand alone as a [free
morpheme](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bound_and_unbound_morphemes "wikilink").\[53\]
Also, Yilan Creole –*suru* can attach to nouns, adjectives, and, among
young generational speakers, verbs. However, older generation speakers
do not accept verbs + -*suru* combinations.

Another affix in Yilan Creole is the Japanese derived –*rasyeru* for
causative forms. However, while Japanese
[inflection](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/inflection "wikilink")
differs between consonant versus vowel ending verbs, the Yilan Creole
suffix does not.\[54\]

### Compound Words

There are four types of [compound
words](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_\(linguistics\) "wikilink")
in Yilan Creole:\[55\]

Type 1: Atayal-derived word + Atayal-derived word (e.g., *hopa*-*la’i*)

Type 2: Atayal-derived word + Japanese-derived word (e.g.,
*hopa*-*tenki*)

Type 3: Japanese-derived word + Atayal-derived word (e.g.,
*naka*-*lukus*, *kako*-*balay*)

Type 4: Japanese-derived word + Japanese-derived word (e.g.,
*naka*-*pangcyu*, *unme*-*zyoto*)

While Type 1 compound words occur in Atayal, Japanese does not have
occurrences of Type 4 compound words in its own language, suggesting
that Type 2, 3, and 4 compound words are creations of Yilan
Creole.\[56\]

### Pronouns

A chart detailing Yilan Creole
[pronouns](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/pronoun "wikilink"):\[57\]

|                           |                    |                           |
| ------------------------- | ------------------ | ------------------------- |
|                           | Singular           | Plural                    |
| The first-person pronoun  | wa/waha            | watati/wahatati           |
| The second-person pronoun | su/anta            | Antatati                  |
| The third-person pronoun  | hiya/zibun/zin/are | zintati/zibuntati/aretati |

The forms of pronouns are Japanese derived. However, in comparison to
both Atayal and Japanese, Yilan Creole has a simplified pronominal
system. The creole distinguishes pronouns between
[person](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammatical_person "wikilink")
and
[number](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammatical_number "wikilink").
Yilan does not distinguish between
[case](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammatical_case "wikilink"), bound
or free pronouns, nor
[inclusive](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clusivity "wikilink") versus
[exclusive](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clusivity "wikilink") pronouns
as Atayal does. It also does not distinguish between
[gender](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammatical_gender "wikilink")
and [degree of
politeness](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politeness_and_respect_\(Japanese_language\) "wikilink")
as Japanese does.\[58\]

### Demonstratives

[Demonstratives](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demonstrative_\(linguistics\) "wikilink")
in Yilan Creole derive from Japanese.\[59\]

### Adjectives and Adverbs

[Adjectives](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adjective "wikilink") and
[adverbs](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/adverb "wikilink") in Yilan
Creole derive from both Japanese and Atayal.\[60\] Ayatal adjectives are
primarily used for colors and subjective feelings. Unlike Japanese,
adjectives in the creole languages are not inflected and tense is
expressed through temporal adverbs.\[61\] Adjectives in Yilan Creole may
also act as adverbs when modifying verbs. For example, the word *lokah*
‘good, strong’ “functions as an adjective when describing *anta* ‘you’
in the phrase *lokah anta* ‘ you (are) strong’ … while *lokah* functions
as an adverb as in *lokah benkyo* ‘to study hard’”.\[62\]

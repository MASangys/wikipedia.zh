**寒溪語**（）或稱**宜蘭克里奧爾語**、**日語客里謳**\[1\]，是一種基於[日語的](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/日語 "wikilink")[克里奧爾語](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/克里奧爾語 "wikilink")。這種語言出現於1930、1940年代，由於[日本殖民政府在台灣推行強力的](../Page/台灣日治時期.md "wikilink")[日語教育](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/皇民化運動#臺灣 "wikilink")，[日語逐漸成為山區](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/日語 "wikilink")[各族原住民之間的共通語](../Page/臺灣原住民族.md "wikilink")，[宜蘭縣部分](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/宜蘭縣 "wikilink")[泰雅族](../Page/泰雅族.md "wikilink")甚至喪失了[族語能力](../Page/泰雅語.md "wikilink")，改以日語為母語。根據一位1974年出生的寒溪語母語者，其詞彙庫中有70%為日語來源、30%為[泰雅語](../Page/泰雅語.md "wikilink")，而其文法則和兩種語言都不太相似\[2\]。

It is incomprehensible to both [Japanese](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_language "wikilink") and [Atayal](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atayal_language "wikilink") native speakers.\[3\] The [creole](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creole_language "wikilink") was identified in 2006 by Chien Yuehchen and Sanada Shinji, however its existence is still largely unknown.\[4\]\[5\] It was named by Sanada and Chien for its location.\[6\]由於近年來[中華民國國語](../Page/中華民國國語.md "wikilink")的普及，使得寒溪語受到極大的威脅\[7\]。

## 性質

寒溪語作為克里奧語，其上層語言為日語，而底層語言為泰雅語\[8\]。

寒溪語為以[賽德克語](../Page/賽德克語.md "wikilink")都達群的單詞及語法為基礎，再加入大量的[日語](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/日語 "wikilink")、[賽考利克泰雅語](../Page/賽考利克泰雅語.md "wikilink")等語種之詞語與語法，經[語言石化過程混合而成的新語種](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/語言石化 "wikilink")，謂之台灣的[克里奥尔语](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/克里奥尔语 "wikilink")（或稱日語客里謳）。\[9\]\[10\]\[11\]

語言學資料庫[Glottolog將寒溪語歸類在日本語族下](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glottolog "wikilink")\[12\]。

## History

The island of [Taiwan was annexed by Japan](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_under_Japanese_rule "wikilink") under the [Shimonoseki Treaty](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Shimonoseki "wikilink") of 1895 until 1945.\[13\]\[14\] Control over the island lasted about fifty years.\[15\] During the latter period of this time, [Imperial Japan](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empire_of_Japan "wikilink") enforced Taiwanese [assimilation](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_assimilation "wikilink") to Japanese language and culture.\[16\] As a result of this contact between the [Atayal](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atayal_language "wikilink") and [Japanese](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_language "wikilink") languages, Yilan Creole surfaced.\[17\] Taiwanese people began attending schools taught in Japanese where all non-Japanese languages were banned, and by 1944 over 77% of Taiwanese were capable in speaking Japanese.\[18\]\[19\] Language reforms, name changes, and laws regarding social customs were among the reforms instilled by the Japanese Imperial government.\[20\] Many are still competent in Japanese today where it is sometimes used as a [lingua franca](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/lingua_franca "wikilink").\[21\]\[22\] Although China attempted to rid influence of Japan in Taiwan after the [surrender of Japan](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/surrender_of_Japan "wikilink") in 1945, the impact on language and culture in Taiwan is still largely evident.\[23\] [Taiwan_ROC_political_division_map_Yilan_County.svg](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Taiwan_ROC_political_division_map_Yilan_County.svg "fig:Taiwan_ROC_political_division_map_Yilan_County.svg") Atayal features surfaced in the Japanese language spoken in Taiwan, eventually becoming a [pidgin](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/pidgin "wikilink") before it fully developed into a [creole](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creole_language "wikilink"), and the language is now currently the only known Japanese-based creole on the island, and possibly in the world.\[24\]\[25\]

## Geographic Distribution

The [creole](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creole_language "wikilink") is spoke in [Yilan County](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yilan_County,_Taiwan "wikilink") in Eastern Taiwan, mainly in Tungyueh Village, Chinyang Village, Aohua Village, and Hanhsi Village, with evidence of differences [varieties](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variety_\(linguistics\) "wikilink") of the creole found in each.\[26\]\[27\] Although the exact number of Yilan Creole speakers is unknown, it is likely less than the total population of the four villages, which is 3,000.\[28\]\[29\] One estimate is 2,000 to 3,000 speakers of Yilan Creole currently exist.\[30\] While the creole is currently used among all generations,\[31\] younger generations are receiving less exposure to it, causing the language to become [endangered](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endangered_language "wikilink").\[32\] While older speakers may not be fluent in [Mandarin](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese "wikilink"), younger generations are consistently using Mandarin more.\[33\]

Japanese language still has some influence in Taiwan society today. Japanese based Internet sites in Japanese are viewed by Taiwanese, with “few other countries \[producing\] as much information in Japanese as Taiwan”.\[34\] Evidence of Japanese signboards, particularly with the use of the Japanese [hiragana](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/hiragana "wikilink") character の (pronounced “no”) may be seen in Taiwan.\[35\] Taiwanese citizens who received Japanese education during the annexation of the nation still speak Japanese fluently today.\[36\] There are three generations of Yilan Creole speakers, with the older and middle generations using the creole significantly more than the younger generation.\[37\]

In Tungyueh Village, younger generations seem to have lost the Yilan Creole, while there are still fluent younger generation speakers of the Creole in Aohua.\[38\] After a push for preserving a more traditional and pure sense of [Atayal heritage](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atayal_people "wikilink"), the Yilan Creole that is imbued with Japanese features was removed from language examinations. This further instilled a tendency toward Mandarin and a push away from Yilan Creole in young speakers.\[39\] While older generations prefer to speak Japanese or Yilan-Creole with people of the same age, they will often use Atayal or Mandarin mixed with Yilan Creole when speaking with younger generations; younger generations will similarly prefer Mandarin with same-age speakers, but may use Yilan Creole with older generations.\[40\]

Due to the migration of Japanese from the western part of the Japan to Taiwan in the early half of the twentieth century, with seventy percent of Taiwan’s immigrants being from western Japan, Yilan Creole acquired some of the regional dialects of western Japan. However, these [regional dialects](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialect "wikilink") are still used unconsciously.\[41\]

寒溪語繼承了日語輔音中的[濁](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/清濁音 "wikilink")[塞音](../Page/塞音.md "wikilink")（、和）、[濁齒齦擦音](../Page/濁齒齦擦音.md "wikilink")、[清齦顎擦音](../Page/清齦顎擦音.md "wikilink")、[清齒齦塞擦音](../Page/清齒齦塞擦音.md "wikilink")，以及齦顎塞擦音和；但未保留[清雙脣擦音](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/清雙脣擦音 "wikilink")和[小舌鼻音](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/小舌鼻音 "wikilink")\[43\]。

Atayal Consonants the Yilan Creole has inherited include the [glottal stop](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/glottal_stop "wikilink") \[ʔ\], alveolar [liquid](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_consonant "wikilink") \[l\], [velar](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velar_consonant "wikilink") fricative \[x\], and the liquid \[l\]. It does not have the uvular stop \[q\] of Atayal. Some other features that Yilan Creole adopted from Atayal are the consonants \[t\] and \[k\] can occur word-finally, velar nasal \[ŋ\] can appear in the word-initial also word-final position, and fricatives \[s\], \[x\] and \[h\] can occur in word-final position. In some Japanese-based words, \[l\] has taken place of \[r\], such as *suware* ‘to sit’ becoming *suwale* ‘to sit’ in Yilan Creole. The [phoneme](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/phoneme "wikilink") \[l\] similarly does this with \[d\] in many Japanese based words.\[44\]

### Vowels

Yilan Creole [vowels](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/vowel "wikilink") consist of \[a\], \[i\], \[u\], \[e\] and \[o\] which derive from both Japanese and Atayal, but also \[ə\] which derives specifically from Atayal.\[45\] However, \[u\] in Yilan Creole resembles the [rounded](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roundedness "wikilink") \[u\] of Atayal instead of the unrounded \[ɯ\] in Japanese.\[46\]

Long consonants and vowels from Japanese words are often shortened in Yilan Creole, where *gakkô* ‘school’ in Japanese becomes *gako* ‘school’ in Yilan Creole.\[47\]

### Stress

[Stress](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress_\(linguistics\) "wikilink") in Yilan Creole falls on the final syllable as it does in the Atayal language.\[48\]

## Grammar

### Morphology

Although Yilan Creole verbs derive from Japanese and Ayatal, [verb conjugation](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation "wikilink") patterns uniquely differ in some aspects. [Tense](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tense–aspect–mood "wikilink") is noted through the use of [affixes](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/affix "wikilink") and temporal adverbs together. Ayatal based verbs will still use Japanese affixation.\[49\] Some processes of negation in Yilan Creole use Japanese derived forms to accommodate the [realis mood](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/realis_mood "wikilink") that is part of Atayal grammar.\[50\]

### Syntax

The [word order](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/word_order "wikilink") of Yilan Creole is SOV. It follows Japanese sentence structure, however, there is evidence of Mandarin-based SVO sentences as well, particularly in younger speakers.\[51\]

## Vocabulary/Lexis

In Yilan Creole, phonological forms of words are derived from Japanese, while the [semantic](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantics "wikilink") properties are derived from Atayal.\[52\] According to a study on Yilan Creole in Tungyueh Village by Zeitoun, Teng, and Wu, “the proportion of Atayal-derived words in the Yilan Creole basic vocabulary is 18.3%, and that of Japanese-derived words is 35.6%. Both Atayal-derived words and Japanese-derived words can be used for 33.8% of the items”.\[53\] [Mandarin](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese "wikilink") and [Southern Min](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Min "wikilink") words also exist, but far less. Older generation speakers tend to use Atayal and Japanese variants more often than younger generational speakers, who prefer Mandarin variants, when they exist. Mandarin based words lose their tone in Yilan Creole.\[54\]

Many Atayal words relating to nature, animals, and plants survived in the creole. Vocabulary of most concepts such as these related to traditional [Atayal](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atayal_people "wikilink") and [Seediq](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seediq_people "wikilink") life and culture are retained in Yilan Creole.\[55\]

### Suffixes

The verb [suffix](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/suffix "wikilink") *–suru* in Yilan Creole**',** derived from the Japanese verb ‘to do’, is similar to its Japanese counterpart, except in that it is a [bound morpheme](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bound_and_unbound_morphemes "wikilink") while the Japanese *-suru* can stand alone as a [free morpheme](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bound_and_unbound_morphemes "wikilink").\[56\] Also, Yilan Creole –*suru* can attach to nouns, adjectives, and, among young generational speakers, verbs. However, older generation speakers do not accept verbs + -*suru* combinations.

Another affix in Yilan Creole is the Japanese derived –*rasyeru* for causative forms. However, while Japanese [inflection](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/inflection "wikilink") differs between consonant versus vowel ending verbs, the Yilan Creole suffix does not.\[57\]

### Compound Words

There are four types of [compound words](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_\(linguistics\) "wikilink") in Yilan Creole:\[58\]

Type 1: Atayal-derived word + Atayal-derived word (e.g., *hopa*-*la’i*)

Type 2: Atayal-derived word + Japanese-derived word (e.g., *hopa*-*tenki*)

Type 3: Japanese-derived word + Atayal-derived word (e.g., *naka*-*lukus*, *kako*-*balay*)

Type 4: Japanese-derived word + Japanese-derived word (e.g., *naka*-*pangcyu*, *unme*-*zyoto*)

While Type 1 compound words occur in Atayal, Japanese does not have occurrences of Type 4 compound words in its own language, suggesting that Type 2, 3, and 4 compound words are creations of Yilan Creole.\[59\]

### 代名詞

寒溪語[代名詞](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/代名詞 "wikilink")：\[60\]

|      |                    |                           |
| ---- | ------------------ | ------------------------- |
|      | 單數                 | 複數                        |
| 第一人稱 | wa/waha            | watati/wahatati           |
| 第二人稱 | su/anta            | Antatati                  |
| 第三人稱 | hiya/zibun/zin/are | zintati/zibuntati/aretati |

The forms of pronouns are Japanese derived. However, in comparison to both Atayal and Japanese, Yilan Creole has a simplified pronominal system. The creole distinguishes pronouns between [person](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammatical_person "wikilink") and [number](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammatical_number "wikilink"). Yilan does not distinguish between [case](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammatical_case "wikilink"), bound or free pronouns, nor [inclusive](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clusivity "wikilink") versus [exclusive](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clusivity "wikilink") pronouns as Atayal does. It also does not distinguish between [gender](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammatical_gender "wikilink") and [degree of politeness](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politeness_and_respect_\(Japanese_language\) "wikilink") as Japanese does.\[61\]

### Demonstratives

[Demonstratives](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demonstrative_\(linguistics\) "wikilink") in Yilan Creole derive from Japanese.\[62\]

### 形容詞與副詞

[Adjectives](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adjective "wikilink") and [adverbs](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/adverb "wikilink") in Yilan Creole derive from both Japanese and Atayal.\[63\] Ayatal adjectives are primarily used for colors and subjective feelings. Unlike Japanese, adjectives in the creole languages are not inflected and tense is expressed through temporal adverbs.\[64\] Adjectives in Yilan Creole may also act as adverbs when modifying verbs. For example, the word *lokah* ‘good, strong’ “functions as an adjective when describing *anta* ‘you’ in the phrase *lokah anta* ‘ you (are) strong’ … while *lokah* functions as an adverb as in *lokah benkyo* ‘to study hard’”.\[65\]

## 文字

寒溪語使用[訓令式羅馬字以及泰雅語拉丁字母書寫](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/訓令式羅馬字 "wikilink")。\[66\]

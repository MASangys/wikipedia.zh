[Gianlorenzo
Bernini](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gianlorenzo_Bernini "wikilink"),
1652\]\]  **聖愛**([古希臘語](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/古希臘語 "wikilink") ,
*agapē*) 是個
[Greco](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greeks "wikilink")-[Christian](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian "wikilink")
term，指的是愛,「愛與仁慈的最高境界」和「上帝對人類及人類對上帝的愛」。\[1\]
這個詞不要與[philia和](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/philia "wikilink")[兄弟的愛混淆](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brotherly_love_\(philosophy\) "wikilink")，因為它包含了一種普遍的、無條件的愛，無論在什麼情況下都會超越並持續存在。名詞形式首先出現在
[Septuagint](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Septuagint "wikilink"),
但動詞形式可以追溯到[荷馬](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homer "wikilink"),
直譯為[感情](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/感情 "wikilink"),
如「問候情感」和「表達對死者的感情」。\[2\]
其他古代作家使用這個詞來表達對配偶或家庭的愛，或對某一特定活動的喜愛，與[情慾](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/情欲 "wikilink")
(一種性慾的情感)不同。

Within Christianity, *agape* is considered to be the [love originating
from God](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/love_of_God "wikilink") or
[Christ](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christ "wikilink") for
mankind.\[3\] In the [New
Testament](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Testament "wikilink"), it
refers to the covenant love of God for humans, as well as the human
reciprocal love for God; the term necessarily extends to the love of
one's fellow man.\[4\] Some contemporary writers have sought to extend
the use of *agape* into non-religious contexts.\[5\] \[6\]

The concept of *agape* has been widely examined within its Christian
context.\[7\] It has also been considered in the contexts of other
religions,\[8\] [religious
ethics](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/religious_ethics "wikilink"),\[9\]
and science.\[10\]

## 早期應用

在多神論希臘文學中很少出現聖愛這個詞。 [Bauer's
Lexicon提到了墳墓一詞](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bauer's_Lexicon "wikilink")，最有可能紀念一個被他的國家“高度尊敬”的多神軍官。\[11\]

的一名記者說
[約翰福音3:16是最著名的聖經經文之一](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_3:16 "wikilink")，因為它被讓維是[基督教中心思想的總結](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity "wikilink")。簡稱為「福音」。

聖愛這個詞在後來的基督徒作家中被廣泛使用，作為特別表示基督徒的愛或
[慈善](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/charity_\(virtue\) "wikilink")
(), 甚至作為上帝自己的詞。 The expression "God is love" (ὁ θεὸς ἀγάπη ἐστίν) occurs
twice in the [New
Testament](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Testament "wikilink"): [1
John](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Epistle_of_John "wikilink") .
*Agape* was also used by the early Christians to refer to the
self-sacrificing love of God for humanity, which they were committed to
reciprocating and practicing towards God and among one another (see
*[kenosis](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/kenosis "wikilink")*).

*Agape* has been expounded on by many Christian writers in a
specifically Christian context. C. S. Lewis uses *agape* in *[The Four
Loves](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Four_Loves "wikilink")* to
describe what he believes is the highest level of love known to
humanity: a selfless love that is passionately committed to the
well-being of others.<ref name=Kreeft>Kreeft, Peter.
["Love"](http://www.catholiceducation.org/articles/apologetics/ap0019.html).
Retrieved May 22, 2009.

  - </ref>

The Christian use of the term comes directly from the canonical
[Gospels](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gospel "wikilink")' accounts of
the teachings of
[Jesus](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesus "wikilink"). When asked what
was the [great
commandment](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Commandment "wikilink"),
"Jesus said unto him, Thou shalt love the Lord thy God with all thy
heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy mind. This is the first
and great commandment. And the second *is* like unto it, Thou shalt love
thy neighbour as thyself. On these two commandments hang all the law and
the prophets." () In
[Judaism](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judaism "wikilink"), the first
"love the  thy God" is part of the
[Shema](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shema_Yisrael "wikilink").

[Bloch-SermonOnTheMount.jpg](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bloch-SermonOnTheMount.jpg "fig:Bloch-SermonOnTheMount.jpg"),
1877\]\] In the [Sermon on the
Mount](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sermon_on_the_Mount "wikilink"),
Jesus said:

[Tertullian](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tertullian "wikilink")
remarks in his 2nd century defense of Christians that Christian love
attracted pagan notice: "What marks us in the eyes of our enemies is our
loving kindness. 'Only look,' they say, 'look how they love one another'
" (*[Apology](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apologetics "wikilink")*
39).

Anglican theologian [O.C.
Quick](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliver_Chase_Quick "wikilink")
writes that this *agape* within human experience is "a very partial and
rudimentary realization," and that "in its pure form it is essentially
divine."

In the [New
Testament](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Testament "wikilink"), the
word *agape* is often used to describe God's love. However, other forms
of the word are used in a negative context, such as the various forms of
the verb
*[agapaō](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/wikt:ἀγαπάω "wikilink")*.
Examples include:

  - — "for Demas hath forsaken me, having loved \[*agapēsas*\] this
    present world...".

  - — "For they loved \[*ēgapēsan*\] the praise of men more than the
    praise of God."

  - — "And this is the condemnation, that light is come into the world,
    and men loved \[*ēgapēsan*\] darkness rather than light, because
    their deeds were evil."

[Karl Barth](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Barth "wikilink")
distinguishes *agape* from
*[eros](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eros_\(concept\) "wikilink")* on
the basis of its origin and unconditional character. In *agape*,
humanity does not merely express its nature, but transcends it. *Agape*
identifies with the interests of the neighbor "in utter independence of
the question of his attractiveness" and with no expectation of
reciprocity.\[12\]

### Meal

The word *agape* is used in its plural form (*agapai*) in the New
Testament to describe a meal or feast eaten by early Christians, as in
[Jude](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistle_of_Jude "wikilink")  and
[2nd
Peter](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Epistle_of_Peter "wikilink")
.

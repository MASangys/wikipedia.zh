**帽兜**是一种[帽子](../Page/帽子.md "wikilink")，覆盖大部分头部与颈部，有时甚至覆盖脸部。帽兜主要覆盖头部的顶部与侧部。

## 词源

英语“hood”是“covering”，源自原始日耳曼语\*hodaz。15世纪开始采用现在的拼写，指出长元音。1905年开始有了汽车引擎盖的含义。\[1\]

## 历史与描述

Historically, hoods were either similar to modern hoods, often forming
part of a [cloak](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/cloak "wikilink") or
[cape](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/cape "wikilink"), or a separate
form of headgear. Hoods with short capes, called
[chaperons](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/chaperon_\(headgear\) "wikilink")
in French, were extremely common in medieval Europe, and later evolved
into big hats. Soft hoods were worn by men under hats. Hoods have also
been used as part of uniforms for organizations such as the [Ku Klux
Klan](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ku_Klux_Klan "wikilink").

A hood to hide or control the wearer often covers the whole head, with
the result that the wearer can see little or nothing, like a
[blindfold](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/blindfold "wikilink"), or it
can be to prevent identification of the wearer. It may be used on or by
a person who has been arrested or
[kidnapped](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/kidnap "wikilink"), or about
to suffer [judicial
execution](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/judicial_execution "wikilink");
this practice is known as
**[hooding](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/hooding "wikilink")**. The
hood may be simply a
[bag](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/bag "wikilink"); it may be intended
to be, and/or experienced as, humiliating (see [hood
event](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/hood_event "wikilink")).

Traditional women's hoods varied from close-fitting, soft headgear (e.g.
[snood](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snood_\(headgear\) "wikilink")) to
stiffened, structured hoods (e.g. [gable
hood](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/gable_hood "wikilink")) or very
large coverings made of material over a frame which fashionable women
wore over towering wigs or hairstyles to protect them from the elements
(e.g.
[calash](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/calash_\(hood\) "wikilink")).

Today, fashion hoods are generally soft headcoverings which form part of
robbing a larger garment (e.g. an
[overcoat](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/overcoat "wikilink"),
[shirt](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/shirt "wikilink") or
[cloak](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/cloak "wikilink"); an exception is
a [rain hood](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/rain_hood "wikilink") which
is not part of a larger garment). They can be pulled up over the head
when needed, or left to hang down the back when not. They may also be
detachable to turn a winter overcoat into a summer one, or may be
designed to be folded or rolled into a small pocket in the neck of the
garment when not in use.

## 类型

The [Inuit](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inuit "wikilink") peoples of
the [Arctic](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic "wikilink") were
expert clothing manufacturers, and the women's
[anorak](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/anorak "wikilink"), technically
called an *[amauti](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/amauti "wikilink")*,
features a large hood used to shelter an infant on its mother's back.

In [Japan](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "wikilink") hoods covered
with chainmail or armour plates [(*tatami
zukin*)](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commons:Category:Tatami_zukin "wikilink")
were worn by [samurai](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/samurai "wikilink")
warriors and their retainers.

[Scuba divers](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scuba_diving "wikilink")
who dive in cold water often wear
[neoprene](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/neoprene "wikilink") hoods for
[thermal
insulation](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/thermal_insulation "wikilink").
They cover the whole head and neck except the face.

### 学位服

A
**[hood](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_dress#Hood "wikilink")**
is a component of [academic
dress](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/academic_regalia "wikilink") that
is an often bright and decorative garment worn over a gown and used only
at graduations or on other special occasions. The shape of hoods of
universities and colleges in the
[UK](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/UK "wikilink") and in many
[Commonwealth](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth "wikilink")
countries have been derived from those prescribed at the Universities of
Oxford and Cambridge.
[Oxford](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Oxford "wikilink")
bachelors and masters use a 'simple' (or 'Burgon' shape) consisting of
hood with a [cowl](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/cowl "wikilink")
(headcovering) but without a
[cape](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/cape "wikilink"), whereas the
[University of
Cambridge](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Cambridge "wikilink")
uses a 'full' shape, with both cowl and square cape and substantial
[liripipe](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/liripipe "wikilink") for all
hoods. Other English universities use one of these patterns or adapt
them; for example, the [University of
London](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_London "wikilink")
uses the full Cambridge shape but with rounded corners to the cape.

Newer universities, such as the [University of
Kent](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Kent "wikilink") use a
hood with triangular cape but with no cowl, and with a distinctive V
shaped segment denoting the
[faculty](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/faculty_\(division\) "wikilink").
The hoods of the [University of
Aberdeen](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Aberdeen "wikilink")
have a rounded flat cape but no cowl. The pattern of hoods in the
[US](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/US "wikilink") largely follow an
intercollegiate code. The length of the hood and the width of its velvet
trim indicate the academic achievement level of the wearer; the color of
the trim indicates the discipline/field in which the degree is held; and
the lining of hoods in academic dress represents the particular
institution from which the degree was earned.

戈戈舞靴是1960年代中期首次推出的低腳跟風格的女性時尚靴子。最初的戈戈舞靴（1964年由[安德烈·庫雷熱](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/安德烈·庫雷熱 "wikilink") (André Courrèges) ）設計是白色、低腳跟和中小腿高的舞靴，這種特定樣式有時被稱為庫雷熱靴子。包括在1960年代和1970年代非常流行的膝高跟方頭靴，配以高跟鞋。以及許多樣式，包括小貓高跟鞋版和白色以外的顏色。

## Etymology

The term *go-go* is derived from the [French](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language "wikilink") expression *[à gogo](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/wikt:à_gogo#French "wikilink")*, meaning "in abundance, galore",\[2\] which is in turn derived from the ancient French word *la gogue* for "joy, happiness".\[3\] The term "go-go" has also been explained as a 1964 [back-formation](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/back-formation "wikilink") of the 1962 slang term "go", meaning something that was "all the rage"; the term "[go-go dancer](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/go-go_dancing "wikilink")" first appeared in print in 1965.\[4\] The go-go boot is presumed to have been named after the dance style.\[5\]\[6\]

## 1960s

Fashion boots were revived in the early 1960s by designers such as [Beth Levine](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beth_Levine_\(fashion_designer\) "wikilink"), although at first they featured fashionable high heels such as the [stiletto](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/stiletto_heel "wikilink") and [kitten heels](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/kitten_heel "wikilink").\[7\] The earliest go-go boots were mid-calf, white and flat-heeled, as seen in the work of the designer [André Courrèges](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/André_Courrèges "wikilink"), who is often credited with creating the style.\[8\]\[9\] The simple minimalism of the Courrèges boot was easily and widely reproduced for the mass market.\[10\]\[11\] Courrèges boots provided the foundation for the development of the go-go boot, which increasingly came higher up the leg and was made in alternative colours.\[12\]\[13\] While remaining low-ish, the heel also became higher and chunkier.\[14\] The earliest Courrèges boots were made of leather, such as [kidskin](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/kidskin "wikilink") or [patent leather](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/patent_leather "wikilink"),\[15\] but many of the subsequent versions and copies were made in [PVC](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/PVC "wikilink"), [vinyl](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_group "wikilink"), and other plastics.\[16\]

In 1966, the song "[These Boots Are Made for Walkin'](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/These_Boots_Are_Made_for_Walkin' "wikilink")" was released and performed by a go-go boot wearing [Nancy Sinatra](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nancy_Sinatra "wikilink"), who is credited with further popularising the boot.\[17\] [Tim Gunn](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tim_Gunn "wikilink") suggests that Sinatra helped establish the boot as "a symbol of female power".\[18\] Female dancers on the television shows *[Hullabaloo](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hullabaloo_\(TV_series\) "wikilink")* and *[Shindig\!](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shindig! "wikilink")* also wore the short, white boots.\[19\] This led to the boots sometimes being called 'hullabaloo boots,' as in an advertisement run in American newspapers in January 1966 for hullabaloo boots with "kooky heels and zipper backs" for the "Go-Go Getter".\[20\]  [1990s_Revival_Go-go_Boots.jpg](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:1990s_Revival_Go-go_Boots.jpg "fig:1990s_Revival_Go-go_Boots.jpg") 1990年代曾復甦風行再流行過。\[21\]

《[藩篱之钟](../Page/藩篱之钟.md "wikilink")》（英文：[The Division
Bell](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Division_Bell "wikilink")），是英国前卫摇滚乐队[平克·弗洛伊德的第](../Page/平克·弗洛伊德.md "wikilink")14张录音室专辑，1994年3月28日和4月4日分别由[EMI和](../Page/EMI唱片.md "wikilink")[哥伦比亚唱片在英国和美国发行](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/哥伦比亚唱片 "wikilink")。该专辑的音乐主要由吉他手兼歌手[大卫·吉尔摩和键盘手](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/大卫·吉尔摩 "wikilink")[理查德·赖特创作](../Page/理查德·赖特_\(音乐家\).md "wikilink")，这是[理查德·赖特自](../Page/理查德·赖特_\(音乐家\).md "wikilink")1973年专辑《[月之暗面](../Page/月之暗面.md "wikilink")》以来第一次在平克·弗洛伊德的专辑中担任主唱。大卫·吉尔摩的未婚妻与乐队合写了许多有关沟通主题的歌词。录音地点包括乐队的Britannia
Row录音室以及吉尔摩的[阿斯托里亚船上录音室](../Page/阿斯托里亚_\(南达科他州\).md "wikilink")。制作团队包括乐队长久以来的盟友，如制作人、工程师、萨克斯演奏者。

《藩篱之钟》达到了英国和美国排行榜的第一名，但收到了不同评价。紧接着乐队便进行了欧洲和美国的巡演。它在美国发行时获得双铂金[认证](../Page/音乐唱片销售认证.md "wikilink")，并在1999年获得三铂金。专辑藩篱之钟时期未被使用的素材成为平克·弗洛伊德2014年的下一张也是最后一张专辑《无尽之河》的一部分。

## 概念

《藩篱之钟》对待沟通的主题和说话的方法可以解决许多问题。\[1\] In the
Studio广播的主持人Redbeard建议专辑提供“通过令人颤抖的优美的时刻，产生真正超越一切的可能性”。\[2\]
歌曲比如〈Poles Apart〉和〈Lost for
Words〉之类的歌曲已被作为表明平克·弗洛伊德和1985年离队的前乐队成员[罗杰·沃特斯之间的隔阂](../Page/罗杰·沃特斯.md "wikilink")；但是，大卫·吉尔摩否认了这一点，并表示：“人们可以用他们个人的方式创作和叙述一首歌，但在这一点上使罗杰回归已经有点晚了。”\[3\]
专辑名称引用了英国国会宣布表决的藩篱钟。\[4\]{{\#tag:ref|The bell used at the end of the
album is not the bell used in Parliament|group = "nb"}}
鼓手[尼克·马森说道](../Page/尼克·马森.md "wikilink")：“这确实有一些意义，它是关于人们做出选择的是与非。”\[5\]

[东方集团垮台后几年](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/東方集團 "wikilink")，〈A Great Day
for
Freedom〉并列将[柏林墙的倒塌以及随之的战争](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/柏林圍牆倒塌 "wikilink")、种族清洗以及[南斯拉夫战争普遍的精神欢快联系在一起](../Page/南斯拉夫內戰.md "wikilink")。\[6\]最初录制为[英国电信的电视广告的](../Page/英國電信.md "wikilink")[斯蒂芬·霍金声音样本被用在歌曲](../Page/史蒂芬·霍金.md "wikilink")〈Keep
Talking〉之中；\[7\]\[8\][大卫·吉尔摩被霍金参与的广告所感动](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/大卫·吉尔摩 "wikilink")，他联系了广告公司，允许他们使用在录制的唱片之中。\[9\]
马森以为：“从广告中获取创意是政治上不正确的，但这似乎是一个非常相关的部分。”\[10\]
在专辑的最后，大卫·吉尔摩的继子查理听到挂在平克·弗洛伊德经理上的电话听筒，他已经恳求允许他出现在平克·弗洛伊德专辑中。\[11\]

## 录制

[Astoria_(Péniche).jpg](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Astoria_\(Péniche\).jpg "fig:Astoria_(Péniche).jpg")
在1993年1月，[吉尔摩](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/大卫·吉尔摩 "wikilink")、马森和赖特开始在改造后的Britannia
Row录音室即兴创作新的素材。他们招募了贝斯手；\[12\]  according to Mason, "an interesting
phenomenon occurred, which was that Pratt's playing tended to change the
mood of the music we had created on our own".\[13\] Without the legal
problems experienced during production of their 1987 album, *[A
Momentary Lapse of
Reason](../Page/A_Momentary_Lapse_of_Reason.md "wikilink")*, Gilmour was
at ease; if he felt the band were "getting somewhere", he would record
them on a two-track [DAT
recorder](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_Audio_Tape "wikilink").\[14\]\[15\]
At one point Gilmour surreptitiously recorded Wright playing, capturing
material which formed the basis for three pieces of music.\[16\]

After about two weeks the band had around 65 pieces of music. With
engineer [Andy
Jackson](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Jackson_\(recording_engineer\) "wikilink")
back on the team, and [Bob
Ezrin](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bob_Ezrin "wikilink") as
co-producer, production moved to Gilmour's houseboat and recording
studio, *Astoria*. The band listened to and voted on each track, and
whittled the material down to about 27 pieces of music. Eliminating some
tracks, and merging others, they arrived at about eleven songs. Song
selection was based upon a system of points, whereby all three members
would award marks out of ten to each candidate song, a system skewed
somewhat by Wright's decision to award his songs ten points each, and
the other songs no points.\[17\] Wright was not contractually a full
member of the band, a situation which upset him. Wright reflected: "It
came very close to a point where I wasn't going to do the album, because
I didn't feel that what we'd agreed was fair."\[18\] Wright received his
first songwriting credits on any Pink Floyd album since 1975's *[Wish
You Were
Here](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wish_You_Were_Here_\(Pink_Floyd_album\) "wikilink")*.\[19\]

Gilmour's fiancée, [Polly
Samson](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polly_Samson "wikilink"), also
received songwriting credits. Initially, her role was limited to
providing encouragement for her husband, but she later helped Gilmour
write "[High
Hopes](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Hopes_\(Pink_Floyd_song\) "wikilink")"
(a song about Gilmour's childhood and early life in
[Cambridge](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambridge "wikilink")). Her
role expanded to co-writing a further six songs, something which did not
sit well with Ezrin. In an interview for *Mojo* magazine Gilmour
admitted that Samson's contributions had "ruffled the management's
\[feathers\]", but Ezrin later reflected that her presence was
inspirational for Gilmour, and that she "pulled the whole album
together".\[20\] She also helped Gilmour, who, following his divorce,
had developed a
[cocaine](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/cocaine "wikilink")
addiction.\[21\]

Keyboard player [Jon
Carin](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jon_Carin "wikilink") and
drummer/percussionist [Gary
Wallis](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gary_Wallis "wikilink") were
brought in to complete the band before recording began. Five backing
vocalists were also hired, including [Sam
Brown](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sam_Brown_\(singer\) "wikilink"),
and *Momentary Lapse* tour singer [Durga
McBroom](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Durga_McBroom "wikilink"). The
band then moved to Olympic Studios, recorded most of the 'winning'
tracks over the space of a week. After a summer break, they returned to
*Astoria* to record more backing tracks. Ezrin worked on the various
drum sounds, and previous collaborator and orchestral composer [Michael
Kamen](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Kamen "wikilink") provided
the album's string arrangements which were recorded at Abbey Road Studio
Two by Steve McLaughlin.\[22\] [Dick
Parry](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dick_Parry "wikilink") played
saxophone on his first Pink Floyd album for almost 20 years, on "Wearing
the Inside Out", and [Chris
Thomas](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chris_Thomas_\(record_producer\) "wikilink")
was booked to undertake the final mix.\[23\] Between September and
December recording and mixing sessions were held at [Metropolis
Studios](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolis_Studios "wikilink") in
[Chiswick](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiswick "wikilink"), and the
Creek Recording Studios in London. In September, the band performed at a
celebrity charity concert at [Cowdray
House](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cowdray_House "wikilink"), in
[Midhurst](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midhurst "wikilink").\[24\] The
album was mastered at the Mastering Lab in Los Angeles, by Doug Sax and
[James
Guthrie](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Guthrie_\(record_producer\) "wikilink").{{\#tag:ref|See
sleeve notes.|group="nb"}}

Jackson edited unused material from the ''Division Bell ''sessions,
described by Mason as [ambient
music](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/ambient_music "wikilink"), into an
hour-long composition tentatively titled *The Big Spliff,*\[25\] but
Pink Floyd decided not to release it.\[26\] Some of *The Big Spliff* was
used to create the band's final album, *[The Endless
River](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Endless_River "wikilink")*
(2014).\[27\]

### 器乐谱写

With the aid of Gilmour's guitar technician, Phil Taylor, Carin located
some of Pink Floyd's older keyboards from the warehouse in which they
had been stored, including a
[Farfisa](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Farfisa "wikilink") organ. Some
of the sounds
[sampled](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sampling_\(music\) "wikilink")
from these instruments were used on the tracks "[Take It
Back](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Take_It_Back "wikilink")", and
"Marooned".\[28\] Carin was joined on keyboards by Ezrin. [Durga
McBroom](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Durga_McBroom "wikilink")
supplied backing vocals alongside Sam Brown, Carol Kenyan, Jackie
Sheridan, and Rebecca Leigh-White.\[29\]

Gilmour used several styles on the album. "What Do You Want from Me" is
influenced by [Chicago
blues](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago_blues "wikilink"), and
"Poles Apart" contains folksy overtones. Gilmour's improvised guitar
solos on "Marooned" used a [DigiTech
Whammy](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digitech_Whammy "wikilink") pedal
to
[pitch-shift](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitch-shifting "wikilink")
the guitar notes over a full octave. On "Take It Back", he used an
[EBow](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/EBow "wikilink"), an electronic
device which simulates the sound of a [bow on the
strings](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/bowed_guitar "wikilink"), on a
[Gibson J-200](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibson_J-200 "wikilink")
guitar through a Zoom effects unit.\[30\]

## 包装和标题

[缩略图](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Expo_Pink_Floyd_-_Division_Bell.jpg "fig:缩略图")
To avoid competing against other album releases (as had happened with *A
Momentary Lapse*) Pink Floyd set a deadline of April 1994, at which
point they would begin a new tour. By January of that year however, the
band still had not decided on a title for the album. The list of names
being considered included *Pow Wow* and *Down to Earth*. At a dinner one
night, writer [Douglas
Adams](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_Adams "wikilink"), spurred
on by the promise of a payment to his favourite charity, the
[Environmental Investigation
Agency](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_Investigation_Agency "wikilink"),
suggested "the [division
bell](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/division_bell "wikilink")" (used in
the lyrics for "High Hopes"), and the name stuck.\[31\]\[32\]

Longtime Floyd collaborator [Storm
Thorgerson](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Storm_Thorgerson "wikilink")
provided the album artwork. He erected two large metal heads, each the
height of a [double-decker
bus](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/double-decker_bus "wikilink"), in a
field near
[Ely](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ely,_Cambridgeshire "wikilink"). The
sculptures were positioned close together, and photographed in profile,
to give the illusion that not only were they either facing or talking to
each other, they also presented the viewer with a third face. Thorgerson
mentioned the "third absent face" was a reference to [Syd
Barrett](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syd_Barrett "wikilink"). The
sculptures were devised by Keith Breeden, and constructed by John
Robertson. [Ely
Cathedral](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ely_Cathedral "wikilink") is
visible on the horizon.\[33\]\[34\] The pictures were shot in cold
February for optimal lighting conditions.\[35\] Since 2001, the
sculptures are in the [Rock and Roll Hall of
Fame](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame "wikilink")
in [Cleveland](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cleveland "wikilink"),
[Ohio](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ohio "wikilink").\[36\] An
alternate version of the cover photo, featuring two  stone sculptures by
Aden Hynes{{\#tag:ref|See sleeve notes.|group="nb"}}, was featured on
the [compact
cassette](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/compact_cassette "wikilink")
release and the tour brochure.\[37\]

The album was released in the UK and US on CD, vinyl, cassette and
[mini-disc](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mini-Disc "wikilink"), each
with its own format and label-specific design. The artwork inside the CD
[liner notes](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/liner_notes "wikilink")
revolves around a similar theme, with the image of the two heads formed
by various other objects, such as newspapers ("A Great Day for
Freedom"), coloured glass ("Poles Apart"), and [boxing
gloves](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/boxing_glove "wikilink") ("Lost
for Words"). Pages two and three portray a picture from the Chilean
[Cerro Tololo Inter-American
Observatory](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cerro_Tololo_Inter-American_Observatory "wikilink").
The CD tray itself had the name of Pink Floyd printed in
[Braille](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Braille "wikilink") on the left
front side.

## 发行和评价

On 10 January 1994 a press reception to announce *The Division Bell* and
world tour was held at a former [US Naval Air
Station](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coast_Guard_Air_Station_Elizabeth_City "wikilink")
in [North
Carolina](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Carolina "wikilink"), in
the US. A purpose-built [Skyship
600](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skyship_600 "wikilink")
[airship](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/airship "wikilink"),
manufactured in the UK, toured the US until it returned to Weeksville,
and was destroyed by a thunderstorm on 27 June. Pieces of the aircraft
were sold as souvenirs. The band held another reception, in the UK, on
21 March. This time they used an A60 airship, translucent, and painted
to look like a fish, which took journalists on a tour of London. The
airship, which was lit internally so it glowed in the night sky, was
also flown in northern Europe.\[38\]

*The Division Bell* was released in the UK by [EMI
Records](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/EMI_Records "wikilink") on 28
March 1994,{{\#tag:ref|UK EMI EMD 1055 (vinyl), EMI CD EMD 1055
(CD)\[39\]|group="nb"}} and in the US on 4 April,{{\#tag:ref|US Columbia
C 64200 (vinyl), Columbia CK 64200 (CD)\[40\]|group="nb"}}\[41\] and
went straight to \#1 in both countries.\[42\] *The Division Bell* was
certified silver and gold in the UK on 1 April 1994, platinum a month
later and 2x platinum on 1 October. In the US, it was certified gold and
double platinum on 6 June 1994, and triple platinum on 29 January
1999.\[43\]

In the United States the album debuted at number one in the [*Billboard*
200](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Billboard_200 "wikilink") during the
week of April 23, 1994 selling more than 460,000 units, at the time it
was the 12th largest single-week total since *Billboard* began using
[SoundScan](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/SoundScan "wikilink") data in
May 1991 and also became the fifth-largest first-week sales sum back
then.\[44\] The next week it stayed at the top of the chart selling a
little less than half its first-week total, it moved 226,000 units
during its second week on chart.\[45\] The next week sales slid by 30%
from last week's sum selling 157,000 units, despite this sales decrease
the album stayed at number one.\[46\] The following week, on May 14,
1994 *The Division Bell* remained at number one on the *Billboard* 200
and sales declined by 17%.\[47\] On its fifth week on chart it fell off
to the fourth place on the chart. It was present on the *Billboard* 200
for 53 weeks.\[48\] It was certified three times platinum by the
[RIAA](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/RIAA "wikilink") on January 29,
1999 for shipments of three million units.\[49\]

Despite decent sales, *The Division Bell* received mixed reviews. Tom
Sinclair of *[Entertainment
Weekly](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly "wikilink")*
gave it a "D", writing that "avarice is the only conceivable explanation
for this glib, vacuous cipher of an album, which is notable primarily
for its stomach-turning merger of progressive-rock pomposity and New Age
noodling".\[50\] *[Rolling
Stone](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rolling_Stone "wikilink")*{{'s}}
Tom Graves criticised Gilmour's performance, stating that his guitar
solos had "settled into rambling, indistinct asides that are as
forgettable as they used to be indelible ... only on 'What Do You Want
from Me' does Gilmour sound like he cares".\[51\] Nevertheless, the
album was nominated in the 1995 [Brit
awards](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brit_awards "wikilink") for the
"Best Album by a British Artist",\[52\] but lost to
[Blur](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blur_\(band\) "wikilink")'s
*[Parklife](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parklife "wikilink")*. In
March the same year the band was awarded with a
[Grammy](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammy "wikilink") for the "Best
Rock Instrumental Performance" on "Marooned".\[53\]

In
[*Uncut*](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncut_\(magazine\) "wikilink")'s
2011 *Pink Floyd: The Ultimate Music Guide*, Graeme Thomson wrote that
''The Division Bell '' "might just be the dark horse of the Floyd canon.
The opening
[triptych](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/triptych "wikilink") of songs
is a hugely impressive return to something very close to the eternal
essence of Pink Floyd, and much of the rest retains a quiet power and a
meditative quality that betrays a genuine sense of unity."\[54\]

[Uncut](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncut_\(magazine\) "wikilink")
reviewed the album once again in 2014 to celebrate the 20th anniversary
reissue, and in their review praised the album for its production,
citing that the album sounded much "more like a classic Pink Floyd album
than 1983's *[The Final
Cut](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Final_Cut_\(album\) "wikilink")*"
and throughout the album noted the empathy and connection between Wright
and Gilmour, stating that these moments were "at the album's musical
heart".\[55\]

The album was re-issued again with the [Pink Floyd
Records](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pink_Floyd_Records "wikilink")
label on 26 August 2016.

## 巡演

Two days after the album's release, the band's [Division Bell
Tour](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Division_Bell_Tour "wikilink")
began at [Joe Robbie
Stadium](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joe_Robbie_Stadium "wikilink"),
in suburban [Miami](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miami "wikilink"). The
set list began with 1967's "[Astronomy
Domine](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy_Domine "wikilink")",
before moving to tracks from 1987's *[A Momentary Lapse of
Reason](../Page/A_Momentary_Lapse_of_Reason.md "wikilink")*, and *The
Division Bell*. Songs from *[Wish You Were
Here](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wish_You_Were_Here_\(Pink_Floyd_album\) "wikilink")*
and *[The Dark Side of the
Moon](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Dark_Side_of_the_Moon "wikilink")*
featured, as well as *[The
Wall](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Wall "wikilink")*. Backing
musicians included [Sam
Brown](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sam_Brown_\(singer\) "wikilink"),
Jon Carin, Claudia Fontaine, [Durga
McBroom](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Durga_McBroom "wikilink"), [Dick
Parry](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dick_Parry "wikilink"), Guy Pratt,
Tim Renwick, and Gary Wallis. The tour continued in the US through
April, May and mid-June, before moving to Canada, and then returning to
the US in July. As the tour reached Europe in late July, Waters was
invited to join the band, but he declined, and later expressed his
annoyance that some Floyd songs were being performed again in large
venues. On the first night of the UK leg of the tour on 12 October, a
1,200 capacity stand collapsed, but with no serious injuries; the
performance was rescheduled.\[56\]\[57\]

During the tour an anonymous person named
[Publius](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Publius_Enigma "wikilink")
posted a message on an internet newsgroup, inviting fans to solve a
riddle supposedly concealed in the new album. The message was verified
during a show in [East
Rutherford](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giants_Stadium "wikilink"),
where white lights in front of the stage spelled out "Enigma Publius".
During a televised concert at Earls Court in October 1994, the word
"enigma" was projected in large letters on to the backdrop of the stage.
Mason later acknowledged that the [Publius
Enigma](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Publius_Enigma "wikilink") did
exist, but it had been instigated by the record company rather than the
band. As of 2014, the puzzle remains unsolved.\[58\]

The tour ended at [Earls
Court](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earls_Court_Exhibition_Centre "wikilink")
on 29 October 1994, and was the group's final concert performance until
[Live 8](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Live_8 "wikilink"). Estimates
placed the total number of tickets sold at over 5.3 million, and gross
income at about $100 million.\[59\] A live album of the tour, named
*[Pulse](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_\(Pink_Floyd_album\) "wikilink")*,
and a concert video, also named
[Pulse](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_\(Pink_Floyd_album\) "wikilink"),
(which was shot on 20 October 1994) were released in June 1995.\[60\]
